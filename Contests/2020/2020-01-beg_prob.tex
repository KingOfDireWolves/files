	\paragraph{Problem 2.} The Euclidean plane is initially white. On each move, Luciano chooses a system of Cartesian coordinates and paints the region where \(y \geq x^2\) in red. Is it possible for the Euclidean plane to be painted completely red after a finite number of moves?\\[7pt]
	\noindent (A system of Cartesian coordinates may be chosen at any angle or scale, and with any point as the origin, but the two axes must be perpendicular.)

	\paragraph{Problem 3.} Prove that for any positive real number \(c\), there exists a positive integer \(n\) such that the sum of the factors of \(n\) is greater than \(cn\).

	\paragraph{Problem 4.} Let \(\mathbb{R}^+\) denote the set of positive real numbers. Find all functions \(f: \mathbb{R}^{+} \to \mathbb{R}^{+}\) such that \(\lfloor f(x)f(y) \rfloor = \lfloor xy \rfloor\) for all positive real numbers \(x\) and \(y\).
